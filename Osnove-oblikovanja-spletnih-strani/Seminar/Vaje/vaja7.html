Using Embedded Style Sheets

XHTML tags are used to "structure" the contents of Web pages - to package the information into meaningful blocks of text and graphics and to arrange those groupings on the page. Cascading Style Sheets (CSS) are then used to "style" that information - to control its visual appearance.

Paragraphs, for instance, can have first-line indents. They also can be indented from the left and right margins of the page, as is this and the following two paragraphs, which are indented 30 pixels from both margins.

Another type of paragraph styling is text alignment, wherein lines of text are blocked to the left, centered, blocked to the right, or justified between the margins. These offset paragraphs, for example, have justified lines extending to the left and right margins.

The page heading is centered on its line. The horizontal rules, like the offset paragraphs, have left and right margins of 30 pixels.

Alignment styling can also be applied to the page as a whole. The body of this document has a 20-pixel margin around all sides in order to increase the white space surrounding the page.

When aligning graphic images, it is necessary to include them inside a block-level tag such as a paragraph, and to apply styling to the paragraph rather than to the image.

The styling applied to this page is performed with an "embedded" style sheet. An embedded style sheet appears in a "style" section coded in the head of the page. Various types of tag, id, and class selectors are used to style identified tags. An embedded style sheet should be the primary way to apply styling to a page.
